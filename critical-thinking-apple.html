What critical thinking really is

Simple
💡 In a nutshell
Critical thinking just means one thing: does this actually make sense? You draw your thinking as a simple picture, then check if the picture is true.

It is not about being negative. It is about taking an idea apart so you understand it — like opening up an engine to see how it works.

🎯 Why it matters
We are drowning in information, and most of it has some spin on it. Drawing the logic as a clear picture lets you see it — and once you can see it, you can check it.
Detailed

Critical thinking is the act of considering whether something makes logical sense. Despite the word, there is nothing negative about it — to be “critical” is to take an argument apart to understand it better. An “argument” here just means a statement put forward as true, not a fight.

It traces back to Socrates (Socratic questioning), recorded by Plato and expanded by Aristotle. Real critical thinking is grounded in scepticism and evidence — looking at the logic impartially, setting aside bias.

⭐ Key takeaway
You cannot challenge logic you cannot see. Draw it clearly first, then scrutinise it.

Everything in this module comes down to two kinds of logic — cause & effect, and prerequisites — combined like building blocks.

➡️

Cause & Effect — “If… then…”

Simple
  • This is when one thing makes another thing happen.
  • Read it as: “If [this], then [that].”
  • Draw it with a straight-down (vertical) arrow.
✏️ Example
“If I let go of the ball, then it drops.” Letting go is enough to make it drop.

Build one in 4 steps:

  1. Write the cause as a full, true sentence.
  2. Write the effect as a full sentence.
  3. Draw an arrow down.
  4. Say it out loud — does it sound right?
⭐ Remember
Cause & effect = down arrow = “If… then…”. Each box is a full, true sentence.
Detailed

Cause & effect (formally sufficiency logic) is about how outcomes come to be: the cause, by itself, is enough to produce the effect. We read it as “If [cause], then [effect]” and draw a vertical arrow.

The granularity is up to you. “If I push the brake pedal, then the car stops” can be exploded into master cylinder, brake lines, callipers and pads — a mechanic needs that detail; a driver just needs to know the brakes work.

✏️ Worked example
“If I delegate a task, then I won’t get the right outcome.” Both statements stand alone; the vertical arrow asserts the causal link.
⭐ Key takeaway
Always write each box as a complete statement you can agree is true on its own.
🔑

Prerequisite — “In order to… I must…”

Simple
  • Something you need first — but it can’t do the job on its own.
  • Read it as: “In order to [my goal], I must [the thing I need].”
  • Draw it with a sideways (horizontal) arrow.
✏️ Example
“In order to use my phone, I must charge the battery.” Charging is needed — but charging alone doesn’t actually use the phone.

Cause & effect

Does the first thing make the second happen? → down arrow.

Prerequisite

Is the first thing just needed first? → sideways arrow.
⚠️ Watch out
Don’t mix the arrows up. Down = makes it happen. Sideways = needed first.
Detailed

Prerequisites (formally necessity logic) are required for an outcome but cannot cause it alone. A flight needs fuel and started engines — but fuelling the plane doesn’t make it fly.

What counts as a prerequisite depends on your goal. If the goal is “the plane flies,” passengers aren’t a prerequisite. If the goal is “a commercially successful flight,” they probably are.

⭐ Key takeaway
Prerequisites = horizontal arrows, read “in order to… I must…”. Necessary, but not enough by itself.
🧩

Assumptions — the hidden glue

Simple
  • An assumption is something you believe but don’t say out loud.
  • It is the glue that makes a link work.
✏️ Example
“If I go outside, then I’m cold.” The hidden assumption is “it’s cold outside right now.” If it’s warm, the link falls apart.

You can say it two ways:

  • “If [cause] and [assumption], then [effect].”
  • “If [cause], then [effect] because [assumption].”
🎯 Why it matters
When you can see the hidden assumption, you can ask: is that even true? That’s how bad plans get caught early — and where new ideas come from.
Detailed

Assumptions are the things we believe (but rarely state) that make a cause produce its effect. Some are hard rules we all accept (gravity); others are soft rules — reliable but situational (office conventions, “that’s how it’s done here”).

Why this is powerful:

  • It lets you “predict the future” — understand the steps and assumptions and you can forecast knock-on effects.
  • It exposes weak logic before you waste time, money and emotion on it.
  • It reveals fake prerequisites (“we can’t move forward until X”) — is X really necessary?
⭐ Key takeaway
Without surfacing assumptions you’re gambling. They’re the leverage point — challenge them and you change the outcome.
💥

Finding solutions — injections

Simple
  • An injection is a new situation that — if it were true — would make the problem disappear.
  • You find one by listing your assumptions and flipping them.

The steps:

  1. Write the problem as “If… then…”.
  2. List lots of assumptions (aim for 10+). Don’t judge them yet.
  3. Flip each one.
  4. Find the one flip that makes you go “oh — that would fix it!”
✏️ Example
Problem: “If I go on holiday, then my cat will starve.” Assumption: “No one else can feed the cat.” Flip it: “Someone else can feed the cat.” → a cat-sitter! 🐱
⚠️ Watch out
An injection is the what, not the how. Write “the cat can be fed without me” — not “buy a food timer” (that’s the how).
⭐ Remember
The opposite doesn’t have to be extreme. The opposite of “not much money” is “enough money” — not “infinite money.”
Detailed

Once the logic is clear, we attack the problem by breaking the connection. An injection is a state that, if true now, would change the cause–effect (or remove the prerequisite) and dissolve the problem. The how comes later.

This is the move behind big disruptors: Tesla challenged that an electric car must be a modest eco-platform; Netflix challenged renting films one at a time. Each asked: “Does it have to be that way?”

There’s a creativity paradox: you can’t list things that would never occur to you. The fix — list everything that does occur to you (the assumptions), then flip them. Most flips are rubbish; you only need one good one.

✏️ Pricing example
“If we raise prices, then we lose customers.” Injection: “there are reasons to stay with us at a higher price.” Re-read it and the effect changes to “we’ll retain most customers.”
⭐ Key takeaway
List what you believe, reverse it, and hunt for the one flip that — if true — makes the problem vanish.
🛠️

Making it happen — tactics

Simple
  • A tactic is how you’ll make the injection real.
  • The injection is the goal. The tactic is the action.
✏️ Example
Injection: “Someone can feed the cat.” Tactics: ask a neighbour, book a cat-sitter, buy an automatic feeder. 🍽️

Two quick checks for any tactic:

  • Will it actually work? (does it really cause the injection?)
  • Is it worth it? (time, money, effort)
⭐ Remember
Tactics are disposable. If one isn’t working, drop it and try another. Never lose sight of the goal.
Detailed

A tactic is an action carefully planned to achieve a specific end — like a set play in sport. The injection is the end; the tactic is the how.

  • Tactics are chosen options, not obligations — if one heads the wrong way, abandon it.
  • Completing a tactic doesn’t guarantee the outcome — keep one eye on the injection.
  • The effect-vs-tactic line is grey; lean toward describing the outcome.
⚠️ Sanity-check every tactic
Causality: will it actually cause the injection? Feasibility: is it worth the time/effort/cost?
⭐ Key takeaway
Tactics are disposable means to a fixed end. If the tactic stops serving the injection, drop it.
🌳

Bigger pictures — chains & branches

Simple

A single link is great. Sometimes you need more of the picture.

  • Chains — fill in the steps between a cause and a far-off effect.
  • Longer chains — think further ahead (buying a house is just a step toward living happily at home).
  • Branches — one effect can have several causes; one cause can have several effects.
✅ Try this
Take one annoying problem from today. Write it as “If… then…”. List 5 assumptions. Flip them. See what ideas pop up. 🌱
Detailed

Extending a connection captures the wider situation, makes assumptions more specific, and opens up more solutions.

  • Detailed chains turn a big “jump in logic” into several small, reasonable steps (apply for a mortgage → make an offer → finance approved → pay deposit → buy the house).
  • Longer chains extend up and down to catch non-immediate causes and effects — real “long-term thinking”.
  • Branches: several causes feed one effect; one cause branches to several effects (positive and negative).

Focus on the top 1–3 causes, skip trivially-true prerequisites, and there’s no standard shape — but keep every single link correct.

⭐ Key takeaway
Grow the diagram to fit reality, but every single connection must still obey the rules.

Cheat sheet, mistakes & quick quiz

Cheat sheet

  • Cause & Effect → “If… then…” → down arrow → one thing makes another happen.
  • Prerequisite → “In order to… I must…” → sideways arrow → needed first, not enough alone.
  • Assumption → the hidden belief that makes a link work.
  • Injection → a new situation that, if true, fixes the problem (the what).
  • Tactic → how you make the injection real (the how).
⭐ The solving recipe
Draw the link → list 10+ assumptions → flip them into injections → pick one and test it → choose a tactic.

🚧 Common mistakes

  • Half-sentences in boxes (“Quality” → “I’m worried about quality”).
  • Mixing up the two arrows.
  • Skipping the assumptions.
  • Writing an injection as an action (that’s a tactic).
  • Stopping at the obvious assumptions.
  • Loving an idea that doesn’t actually fix the problem.

✅ Quick quiz (no pressure)

  1. Which arrow does cause & effect use — down or sideways?
  2. Finish: “In order to make tea, I must ______.”
  3. What is an assumption, in your own words?
  4. Why list 10+ assumptions before flipping any?
  5. “Buy a food timer” — injection or tactic? Why?
  6. Name the two checks you give a tactic.
