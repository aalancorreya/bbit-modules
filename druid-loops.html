What is a druid loop?

Simple
💡 In a nutshell
A “druid loop” is a problem that keeps coming back. Two opposite choices each cause a new problem that pushes you back to the other one. Round and round. See the loop → break the loop.

Every druid loop has the same parts:

  • Two clashing behaviours at the bottom (you can’t do both — not enough time or money).
  • Cause-and-effect steps rising from each one.
  • A “goal violation” at the top of each side = something bad enough to make you switch.
  • Loop-back arrows — each bad result pushes you toward the opposite behaviour.
⭐ Remember
Behaviour → steps → something bad → switch to the opposite → repeat. No two clashing behaviours? Then it’s probably not a druid loop.
🎯 Keep the feelings in
If a feeling drives the next choice (“I feel guilty, so I…”), it belongs in the map. We’re not robots — and neither is anyone else.
Detailed

A druid is a mapping of a recurring dilemma. It lays out both sides and connects them with small cause-and-effect steps until the loop closes.

The core insight: a recurring problem is not a single bad decision — it’s a cycle. One approach creates a negative side effect, which pushes you toward the opposite approach, which creates its own negative, which pushes you back. If a simple fix existed, it would have been solved long ago.

That switching is costly on its own: progress stalls, people don’t know what to expect, and you start to look unpredictable.

⭐ Don’t strip out emotion
Druids deliberately include emotional language. If a feeling is part of the causal chain, it belongs in the druid — otherwise your solution won’t be realistic.

Spotting a druid loop

Simple

Example 1: make lunch 🥪 vs buy lunch

  • Make it: I lose my evening → I feel annoyed → “Forget it, I’ll just buy lunch.”
  • Buy it: I spend more money → I feel guilty → “I really should make my lunch.”
⚠️ Watch out
It looks too small to bother with. That’s the trap. Small loops quietly waste time and energy over and over.

Example 2: buy cheap vs buy quality 💼

  • Cheap: poor tools → work suffers → morale drops → “Buy quality next time.”
  • Quality: we spend a lot → budget looks bad → finance complains → “Go cheap next time.”
⭐ Remember
You spot a druid loop when you hear the same two arguments come up again and again.
Detailed

Picture a meeting split in two: “We’ve got to do it this way” vs “No — that way.” Both are sure they’re right; neither sees the other side. That stand-off is the signature of a druid loop.

The equipment version is one many organisations live in — buying $12 chairs one week and a $5,000 laptop the next. External events can shove you onto one side once (a budget freeze), but the underlying trend is the loop pulling you back and forth.

⭐ Key takeaway
The structure matters more than the exact path — as long as the behaviours clash and each side climbs to a loop-back goal violation.
🏗️

How to build one

Simple

The steps:

  1. Name the two clashing behaviours.
  2. Start with the side you know best (often the other person’s).
  3. Build the steps: “If I do this, then this happens, then that…”
  4. Check the bad result is bad enough to make someone switch.
  5. Do the other side the same way.
  6. Close the loop: each bad result pushes the opposite behaviour.
⚠️ Watch out
Write behaviours you could actually see someone doing — not vague words like “quality”. “Getting involved in every task” is a behaviour; “quality” is not.

Worked build — managing a team

  • Get involved: team waits for me → work slows → pressure to step back.
  • Leave them to it: they decide without all the info → work goes wrong → lots of redoing → pressure to get involved.
⭐ The “good loop” checks
Real clashing behaviours? Does each step really cause the next? Is each side true now (not a fear)? Is each bad result serious and does it push the opposite behaviour?
Detailed

Build it as a table in your head or on paper:

  1. Identify the mutually-exclusive behaviour pair.
  2. Pick the side whose negatives are most obvious.
  3. Build true “if-then” steps up to a goal violation.
  4. Confirm the goal violation is negative enough to force a switch.
  5. Repeat for the trickier side.
  6. Confirm each goal violation drives the opposite behaviour.
⭐ Key takeaway
A robust druid has conflicting behaviours, true if-then steps, real (not feared) negatives on both sides, and goal violations that each push to the opposite behaviour.

How to fix a druid loop

Simple
💡 The big secret
Don’t try to delete the whole loop. The behaviours exist for good reasons. You keep one — you just stop the bad side effect and the switching.

You do this by breaking ONE link with an injection (a clever change to reality).

The steps:

  1. Pick one link to break.
  2. Ask why it happens (“…because…”) and list the reasons.
  3. Flip a reason into an idea (an injection) and pick one.
  4. Check it really works — slot it in and see if the bad result shrinks.
⭐ Remember
Break one link and the whole loop can fall apart. You keep the behaviour; you remove the bad bit that fed the cycle.
Detailed

Every step in a druid is a cause-and-effect connection, so you use the injection process from the Critical Thinking program. An injection changes reality so a cause can still happen without producing the same negative effect.

  1. Choose a connection to break (e.g. the “goes wrong → gets worse” link).
  2. Raise assumptions on it (“…because…”).
  3. Generate injection ideas and select one.
  4. Check the causality still holds with the injection slotted in.

If it holds, the change rolls up the rest of the chain, softening each step to something acceptable. The pressure to switch disappears, so the loop stops.

⭐ Key takeaway
Break one connection with an injection and the whole loop unravels.
🧪

Full example — the magic potion

Simple

The loop: a magic potion makes you strong 💪 — but it turns too many people into newts 🦎. So we stop using it… but then we don’t have enough rocks… so we go back to the potion. 🔁

Fixing it:

  1. Break this link: “If we use the potion, then people turn into newts.”
  2. Why? Maybe everyone is affected and there’s no way to avoid it.
  3. Flip one: “Not everyone is affected — some people are naturally resistant.” (Might already be true!)
  4. Check: resistant people use it → very few newt problems → no reason to stop → loop broken. ✅
⭐ Remember
Aim for good enough, not perfect. Like driving or flying, we accept a small, controlled risk instead of giving up the benefit.
Detailed

The original druid: a potion gives strength but turns too many people into newts, so we decline it — but then we don’t have enough rocks, so we return to the potion.

Raising assumptions on “if we use the potion, then we suffer the side effects”: the potion has effects beyond strength; the Druid can’t make one without side effects; we are vulnerable; there’s no way to mitigate.

The tempting injections (“no side effects at all”) are unrealistic — almost all real medications have side effects. The promising one needs little action because it may already be true: “the people using the potion have very little vulnerability to side effects.”

Follow it up the chain: only mild, rare effects → people shouldn’t turn into newts → very few incidents. The negative drops to an acceptable level and the loop breaks.

⭐ Key takeaway
Chasing a guaranteed 0% outcome (a “unicorn”) can cost more than it’s worth. Reduce the harm enough, then control it.

Cheat sheet, mistakes & quick quiz

Cheat sheet

  • Druid loop = two clashing behaviours, each causing a problem that pushes you to the other.
  • Spot it: the same two arguments keep coming back.
  • Build it: behaviours → steps → bad result → switch → repeat (start with the easy side).
  • Fix it: keep a behaviour, break one link with an injection, check it works.
  • Goal: good enough, not perfect.

🚧 Common mistakes

  • “It’s too small to bother with.”
  • A vague word (“quality”) instead of a real behaviour.
  • Taking feelings out to look “logical”.
  • Using a fear that hasn’t actually happened.
  • Deleting the whole loop instead of breaking one link.
  • Chasing a perfect fix when “good enough” works.

✅ Quick quiz (no pressure)

  1. In one sentence, what makes a druid loop keep repeating?
  2. What two things must the bottom behaviours be?
  3. Why keep feelings in the map?
  4. What is a “goal violation” and what must it do?
  5. Why break one link instead of deleting the whole loop?
  6. Explain “good enough, not perfect” using the potion.
⭐ Well done
Answered a few? You’ve got the core idea. 🎉

📚 Mini glossary

  • Druid loop — a repeating problem drawn as a cycle of two clashing behaviours.
  • Behaviour pair — the two clashing choices at the bottom.
  • Goal violation — a result bad enough to make you switch sides.
  • Loop-back — the arrow that sends you to the opposite behaviour.
  • Injection — a clever change to reality that breaks one link.
  • Good enough — reducing the bad part to a level you can live with.
